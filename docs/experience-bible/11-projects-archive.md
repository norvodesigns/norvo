# Projects — Archive of Artifacts

*The role of this section in the bible: it defines the Projects page as the Observatory's permanent record of made things — a working archive where finished web environments are stored, surfaced, retrieved, and examined as discrete artifacts, and where a visitor crosses cleanly from looking at Norvo's work to standing inside it.*

---

## 1. Where This Page Lives

The Projects page is not a stop along The Ascent. The Ascent is the homepage journey; Projects is a destination reached by choosing **Projects** in the primary navigation, which always reads, in order, **Home / Projects / Services / Contact / Start a Project**. A visitor may arrive here from anywhere — mid-Ascent, from the Observatory, from a deep link — and the page must feel like one consistent room of the same building regardless of where they came from.

That room is the **Archive**: a dedicated wing of the Observatory whose single purpose is to hold the work. It inherits the Observatory's settled state of the four-color system at full harmony. **Graphite #14161A** is the architecture — the deep, matte structure of the archive walls and the seams between things. **Archive White #F4F5F7** is the clean interior light that falls across every surface and makes the work legible. **Norvo Violet #6D5DFB** is the living signal — it marks what is interactive, what is selected, what is alive right now. **Observatory Gold #D8B46A** is the prestige of value — it marks what has been recognized as **Featured** and it edges the most consequential moments of arrival. The **Signature Gradient** (violet to gold, 120°) is reserved: it crowns exactly two things on this page — the archive's title and the moment a Live artifact opens to the real world.

Type is **Geist Mono**, as it is everywhere. Here it wears its Observatory dress, **The Luminous Readout**: open and exact spacing like a fine instrument panel, every level of hierarchy coexisting without any of them shouting, the faintest slow shimmer of the Signature Gradient passing across a few key words — the page title, an artifact's name on hover — like light moving along brushed metal. The visitor never thinks "monospaced." They feel "catalogued, precise, valued."

The page is, before anything else, an answer to a question the Usability Covenant requires it to answer instantly: *Where am I?* The persistent header reading **ARCHIVE** in the Signature Gradient, the primary navigation pinned to its usual place, and the unmistakable sense of a catalogue all say the same thing the moment the page settles: you are looking at Norvo's work, and you can leave for any other page in one obvious move.

## 2. The Governing Metaphor — Artifacts, Not Cards

Most portfolios present work as a grid of cards: equal rectangles, stacked, scrolled past. The Archive rejects that flatness for the same reason the whole site rejects flat pages. Each project is an **artifact** — a made object with weight, an edge, a face, and a back. The Archive's job is to *store* artifacts, *surface* them for consideration, let a visitor *retrieve* one, *examine* it, and finally *enter* it. Those five verbs — store, surface, retrieve, examine, enter — structure everything below.

An artifact is never a photograph of a website pinned to a wall. It is a slim, rigid plate, as if the project's home screen were pressed into a single tile of dark Graphite glass with Archive White light caught along its top and left edges, giving it a real near-edge and a real far-edge. Each plate sits in the archive at a slight, consistent depth offset from its neighbors — the near edge nearer to the visitor, the far edge receding — so the wall of work reads as a shallow three-dimensional shelf, not a printed sheet. The visitor perceives, immediately and without instruction, that these objects could be picked up.

## 3. How Artifacts Are Stored — The Shelf

The default state of the Archive is **the Shelf**: the full set of artifacts at rest.

The Shelf is a single horizontal register of artifact plates, arranged left to right in a calm, evenly spaced row at a comfortable reading height, with a generous band of Archive White light above and below so each plate breathes. The plates are not all identical in size. **Featured** artifacts are stored at a larger scale and slightly nearer the visitor — their near edges sit forward of the unfeatured ones, and a single hairline of Observatory Gold runs along their lower edge, the same precious-touch role gold has carried since Era 2. An unfeatured artifact is stored at the baseline scale, set a half-step further back, with no gold. The hierarchy is spatial and chromatic, never loud: the most important work is simply *closer and warmer*.

Because the Shelf extends beyond the visible frame, the right and left margins of the view hold a faint depth gradient — Graphite deepening toward the edges — that communicates "there is more stored this way." The Shelf does not paginate. It is one continuous shelf the visitor travels along.

Every plate, at rest, shows only its essential record:
- the **project name** in Geist Mono, medium weight, in Archive White;
- a small uppercase tracked-out **status label** — **LIVE** or **PROTOTYPE** — in Norvo Violet, the system signal color, sitting top-left on the plate;
- if applicable, a small **FEATURED** label top-right, in Observatory Gold;
- a single quiet line of discipline beneath the name (e.g. *Laser-engraving e-commerce* / *Marine manufacturing brand* / *Luxury private spaceflight concept* / *Real-depth parallax real estate*).

The face of the plate holds a still, muted impression of the project itself — its composition legible but dimmed, as though the work is sleeping and waiting to be woken. Nothing on the Shelf moves on its own except one thing: the **Pulse**, the slow breathing glow that lives on interactive elements throughout the experience. On the Shelf, the violet status labels carry the faintest Pulse — a slow brightening and settling, perhaps one cycle every several seconds, just enough to say *these objects are live and waiting*.

## 4. How Artifacts Are Surfaced — Travel and Filtering

### 4.1 Traveling the Shelf (desktop)

The visitor moves along the Shelf laterally. As they travel, the artifacts **Parallax**: the near edges of the plates shift more than the far edges, so the whole shelf has the gentle differential motion of real objects passing a real viewpoint, with the faintest lag on the furthest-back plates. The motion is tied one-to-one to the visitor's input and carries momentum — a longer push glides further and decelerates with the weight of objects, never snapping to a stop. This is **The Drift** applied to lateral travel: weightless, breath-paced, already-in-motion when the hand leaves the input.

As an artifact passes through the center of the frame, it **wakes**: its dimmed face brightens by a clear step toward full Archive White legibility, its name picks up the faint Signature Gradient shimmer of The Luminous Readout, and (for unfeatured artifacts) it eases a half-step nearer the visitor, narrowing the depth gap to its Featured neighbors. The instant it leaves center, it settles back. Only one artifact is ever fully awake at a time on the Shelf — the one under consideration — which keeps the eye guided and the page calm.

### 4.2 Sorting and Filtering — The Register

Above the Shelf sits a thin **Register**: a single restrained row of filter controls in Geist Mono, uppercase, tracked out, Observatory-panel style. The Register offers exactly the distinctions the work actually carries — no invented taxonomies:
- **ALL**
- **LIVE** (real client environments currently in the world)
- **PROTOTYPE** (Norvo's in-house interactive concept worlds)
- **FEATURED**

The active filter is marked by violet — its label brightens to full Norvo Violet and carries a Pulse; the inactive labels rest in dimmed Archive White. Choosing a filter does not reload or flash the Shelf. It re-sorts continuously: artifacts that no longer belong **Drift** outward and dim away along the depth gradient at the edges; the surviving set re-spaces itself along the Shelf with the same weighted, decelerating motion, closing the gaps without any plate teleporting. The visitor watches the collection re-compose. A small count — *4 artifacts* — sits at the end of the Register and ticks to its new value in the Era-3 manner (a brief roll of the numeral) so the change is registered, not merely seen.

If a filter yields a set small enough to hold in one frame, the Shelf simply centers that set with extra Archive White breathing room. The Archive never shows an empty Shelf during normal use; the smallest honest set (currently the four canonical projects, in any combination) always renders as a composed, intentional row.

## 5. How an Artifact Is Retrieved — Selection and the Lift

To consider an artifact closely, the visitor selects the awake plate at center (a click, or selection of any plate directly). This is **the Lift**, the retrieval gesture, and it is deliberately distinct from opening the project itself — examining and entering are two separate acts.

On the Lift:
1. The chosen artifact eases forward out of the Shelf toward the visitor and grows to occupy the calm center of the frame — a clean decelerating **Settle**, no bounce, about one beat.
2. The rest of the Shelf does not vanish; it recedes one depth-step and dims toward Graphite, remaining faintly visible at the periphery so the visitor never loses the sense of standing in a larger archive. The Usability Covenant is honored: *Where am I?* — still in the Archive, with one artifact lifted out for examination.
3. As the plate arrives, it **turns**: it rotates a few degrees so the visitor sees both its bright face and a sliver of its Graphite edge, confirming its objecthood, then settles square-on.

The lifted artifact now shows its full **record card**, rendered directly on and beside the plate in The Luminous Readout hierarchy:
- **Name** (largest level, Archive White, faint gradient shimmer).
- **Status** — LIVE or PROTOTYPE — violet label, with one plain sentence of what that status means, so no visitor must guess. For Live: *A real environment, currently live in the world.* For Prototype: *A self-contained concept world, built in-house to demonstrate a way of building.*
- **Featured** — if present, the gold label with one line: *Recognized as defining Norvo work.*
- **Discipline / sector**, **what it is**, and a short **made-by-Norvo** statement of the problem and the move — three or four exact lines, never a wall of text.
- Two clearly separated affordances: **EXAMINE** (open the case study, internal) and **ENTER** (open the live external site or the prototype world). For Prototypes with no separate case study, the two collapse into a single **ENTER WORLD**. The distinction between examining the story and entering the thing is always explicit.

A persistent **Back to Shelf** affordance sits in the same place at all times. Choosing it reverses the Lift exactly: the plate turns square, recedes, and lowers back into its slot on the Shelf while the surrounding archive brightens forward to full presence — the seam closed in the same motion it opened.

## 6. How an Artifact Is Examined — The Case Study

Choosing **EXAMINE** opens the project's case study. This is an internal Norvo page, fully inside the four-color, Geist-Mono, Observatory-dressed system — it is *Norvo telling the story of the work*, not yet the work itself.

The transition is a controlled descent, not a page swap. The lifted plate, already centered, eases forward until its bright face fills the frame and its edges pass beyond the view, so the visitor feels they have moved *into* the artifact's surface. As the face fills, its dimmed home-screen impression resolves to full clarity and the case study composes downward from it: the project becomes the masthead of its own page, and the story unfolds beneath in a single calm vertical reading column with wide Archive White margins — the austere reading posture of Era 1 deliberately recalled, now lit by Observatory harmony, because a case study is fundamentally a document about a made thing.

The case study reads in The Luminous Readout but borrows the **Composed Column** discipline for its body: clear three-step hierarchy, hairline Graphite rules separating movements, labels tracked out. Its movements are consistent across every project so two case studies are never read two different ways:
- **The Record** — name, status, sector, links, and Featured mark.
- **The Problem** — what the client or concept faced, in plain declarative Norvo voice.
- **The Move** — what Norvo designed and why, told through depth/motion/intention.
- **The Result** — for Live work, the real outcome; for Prototypes, the idea proven.
- **The Threshold** — a single closing panel holding the **ENTER** affordance and, beneath it, a quiet **Start a Project** prompt, so the story of finished work flows naturally toward the visitor beginning their own.

Throughout, Norvo Violet marks links and the active reading position; Observatory Gold marks any stat or recognized result (the same value-marking role it carries for the impact stats elsewhere); the Signature Gradient is held back and appears only on the Threshold's ENTER affordance. Scrolling reveals each movement with **The Reveal** — fade up and a short ease into place, in reading order, gentle and brief. Nothing parallaxes aggressively here; a case study is a place to read, and motion stays in service of reading.

## 7. How an Artifact Is Entered — The Threshold

**ENTER** is the page's most consequential act, and it behaves differently for Live work and for Prototype worlds. The difference is intentional and the visitor is always told which they are about to cross.

### 7.1 Entering a Live external site (Mozart Laser, Amtex Marine)

A Live artifact leads out of the Norvo experience entirely, into a real client's real website at its real address (mozartlaser.com, amtexmarine.com). This is a true crossing, and the Archive marks it with honesty and ceremony.

When the visitor chooses ENTER on a Live artifact, the **Threshold** appears as a brief, calm confirmation panel — not a jarring redirect. The panel states plainly: *This is a live client environment. You are leaving the Archive to enter the real world.* The Signature Gradient — held in reserve all page — finally blooms here along the edge of the ENTER affordance, in its second and final sanctioned appearance on the page, because crossing into proven, living, real-world Norvo work is exactly the kind of meaningful arrival the gradient is reserved for. The artifact's bright face then opens outward — its Graphite edges sweeping past the frame as a doorway opens — and the destination resolves through it. Because the visitor is leaving Norvo's domain, this crossing opens the client site in its own space so the Archive remains intact behind them; returning brings them back to the very artifact they left, still lifted, still warm. At no point is a client's brand restyled, framed, or wrapped in Norvo chrome — a Live site is shown as itself, in the world, which is the whole point of its being Live.

### 7.2 Entering a Prototype world (Aurora, Strata)

A Prototype is a **self-contained fictional brand universe** Norvo built in-house — Aurora's luxury private spaceflight concept, Strata's layered real-depth parallax real estate. Each has its own complete identity, and that identity is sovereign: the Norvo four-color palette and Geist-Mono system do **not** extend across the threshold into it. Inside, the prototype is entirely itself.

Entering a Prototype is therefore framed as stepping *into a built world*, not *out to the real one*. The Threshold panel states: *This is a Norvo prototype — a self-contained concept world. You are entering it now.* There is no Signature Gradient bloom here (that is reserved for the brand statement and Live crossings); instead the artifact's face opens and the **last frame of the Archive's light dims to neutral** as the prototype's own palette and atmosphere take over — a clean handoff that visibly signals *you are now in a different world with its own rules*. A small, unobtrusive **Return to Archive** affordance, in the prototype's own visual language so as not to deface it, persists at one corner so the visitor is never trapped inside a walled-off universe. Choosing it dims the prototype world back to neutral and brings the Norvo Archive light and palette back up, depositing the visitor at the artifact they entered — the seam closed symmetrically.

The two crossings are never confused: **Live opens outward to the real world and blooms the gradient; Prototype opens inward to a sovereign built world and hands palette control over to it.** The visitor always knows, before crossing, which kind of door they are opening.

## 8. The Featured Concept Throughout

Featured is not a separate page or a hero banner; it is a *property of an artifact* that expresses itself consistently at every stage. On the Shelf, a Featured artifact is larger, nearer, and gold-edged. In the Register, FEATURED is a first-class filter. On the Lift, the gold label and its one-line meaning appear in the record. In the case study, the gold mark sits in The Record. The thread of Observatory Gold — value, craft, arrival — runs unbroken from the wall to the story, so "this is defining Norvo work" is felt before it is read.

## 9. Mobile — Traversing the Archive by Touch

Mobile is not a reduction of the Archive; it is the same Archive re-choreographed for a held screen and a thumb. Everything above has a precise touch equivalent.

**The Shelf becomes a deck under the thumb.** Rather than a wide lateral register seen at once, mobile presents one artifact plate centered and nearly full-width, with the near edges of the previous and next plates just visible at the left and right margins — so the depth and the "there is more" cue survive. The visitor travels the Shelf by **horizontal swipe**: a swipe carries the same weighted, decelerating Drift as desktop lateral travel, momentum included, and settles the next artifact at center with a clean Settle. The Parallax is re-tendered to tilt and travel together — as a plate moves to center its near edge leads and its face brightens and wakes exactly as on desktop, and a gentle device-tilt gives the centered plate a small live parallax of its near edge against its far edge so the objecthood is felt in the hand. Only the centered plate is awake; neighbors rest dimmed.

**The Register becomes a single row of tap-pills** pinned just below the persistent header. The four filters — ALL / LIVE / PROTOTYPE / FEATURED — sit as tracked-out Geist-Mono pills; the active one carries full Norvo Violet and the Pulse. Tapping a filter re-sorts the deck with the same continuous Drift-and-dim re-composition; the artifact count ticks beside the row. No dropdown, no hidden menu — the filters are always one tap away, satisfying the Covenant.

**The Lift becomes a tap-to-rise.** Tapping the centered plate (or a small EXAMINE/ENTER affordance on it) rises it forward to fill the calm center while the neighboring plates recede and dim, and the plate performs the same few-degree turn to confirm its edge before settling square. The record card composes beneath the plate in a single column sized for reading on a phone, with EXAMINE and ENTER (or ENTER WORLD) as two full-width, clearly separated touch targets and a persistent **Back to Shelf** bar at the top. There is no hover on touch, so the wake-on-center behavior that desktop triggers by passing through the frame is here triggered automatically by a plate reaching center — the active artifact is always the one the visitor has swiped to.

**Examine on mobile** opens the case study as a vertical read — its natural posture — with the plate's face filling the screen and the masthead resolving from it, then the movements (Record, Problem, Move, Result, Threshold) revealing in reading order with The Reveal as the visitor scrolls a single thumb-driven column. Hairline rules separate movements; margins tighten but never crowd; the gold marks and violet links read identically.

**Entering on mobile** is identical in meaning and equally explicit. The Threshold confirmation panel appears full-width: Live states the leaving-to-the-real-world crossing and blooms the Signature Gradient along the ENTER button before opening the client site in its own space; Prototype states the entering-a-built-world crossing and hands the screen over to the prototype's sovereign palette, with the small Return to Archive affordance kept reachable in the prototype's own corner. The two doors stay visibly distinct on the small screen exactly as on desktop.

**Returning** on mobile, from either a Live site or a Prototype world, brings the visitor back to the artifact they left — still lifted, still centered, the deck waiting behind it — so the journey out and back is symmetrical and never disorienting.

## 10. Accessibility and the Reduced-Motion Variant

The Archive honors reduced-motion as a first-class experience, not a stripped one. With motion reduced, the Drift, Parallax, Lift-turn, and Threshold sweeps soften into gentle cross-fades: filtering cross-fades the Shelf from one composed set to the next; selecting an artifact cross-fades it to its lifted record; entering cross-fades to the case study or destination. Every meaning carried by motion — which artifact is awake, that an object is being lifted, that a door is opening outward (Live) or inward (Prototype) — survives in the still composition, the labels, the color roles, and the Threshold's plain words. The status of every artifact (Live / Prototype / Featured), the meaning of each status, and both affordances (Examine / Enter) are always present as legible Geist-Mono text in Archive White, Norvo Violet, and Observatory Gold, never conveyed by color or motion alone. The primary navigation, the ARCHIVE header, and a route to Contact and Start a Project are reachable from every state of the page.

---

## 11. The Seam Back Out

The Archive connects to the rest of the experience without any hard edge. Leaving Projects for another page is always one move on the primary navigation. Every artifact's case study ends on the Threshold panel that offers, beneath ENTER, a quiet path to **Start a Project** — so a visitor who has just examined Norvo's proven work is handed, in the same breath, the door to commissioning their own. The Archive is where looking at the work becomes wanting the work, and the page is built so that wanting has somewhere immediate and obvious to go.
